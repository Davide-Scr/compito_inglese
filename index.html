Welcome

The year 1945 marked an irreversible fracture in human history. This project explores the dramatic shift from the marvel of scientific discovery to the cold reality of mass destruction.

Our Goal

To demonstrate to the class that the existence of nuclear weapons is not merely a distant political or military issue, but the most urgent humanitarian threat of our time.

Curated by: Balzano, Varsallona, Scramuzza.

Why was the bomb created?

The atomic bomb was not born from an intrinsic desire to destroy, but from global terror. During World War II, the Allied scientific community feared that Nazi Germany would be the first to develop a nuclear weapon.

1939: The Warning

Albert Einstein signed a letter to US President Roosevelt, warning him of the destructive potential of nuclear fission.

The Manhattan Project

The US launched a massive secret initiative, gathering the world's brightest minds, including many European refugees.

The Turning Point

Initially meant for deterrence. However, after Germany surrendered, the weapon was used against Japan to force a quick end to the war and establish US post-war supremacy.

What happened in Hiroshima and Nagasaki?

On August 6 (Hiroshima) and August 9, 1945 (Nagasaki), humanity witnessed the annihilating power of the atom. Entire cities were vaporized in mere moments.

The Hibakusha: The Survivors

"We saw a blinding flash, the 'pika', and then we were overwhelmed by the 'don', an indescribable roar. The world suddenly went silent and gray."

The term Hibakusha translates to "explosion-affected people". Their testimonies reveal unimaginable horrors:

  • People literally turned into shadows imprinted on walls.
  • A terrifying radioactive "black rain" falling from the sky, contaminating water and soil.
  • Decades of suffering: radiation sickness (leukemia, tumors), social discrimination, and inescapable psychological trauma.

Their voices serve as our most powerful warning against the use of nuclear weapons.

How did the world change after August 1945?

The use of the bomb ended WWII but immediately ushered in the Cold War and the era of nuclear terror.

The USA and USSR began competing to stockpile tens of thousands of warheads, eventually developing the vastly more destructive Hydrogen Bomb.

Global stability was based on the terrifying certainty that a nuclear attack would lead to the total annihilation of both sides.

In response, civil society, scientists, and the UN began advocating for non-proliferation. The message is clear: global security is not achieved by threatening to destroy the Earth, but by eliminating these weapons.

Our Call: 1,000 Cranes for Disarmament

Do you know the story of Sadako Sasaki? She was two years old when the bomb fell on Hiroshima. At age twelve, she developed leukemia. Remembering an ancient legend, she began folding origami cranes: it is said that anyone who folds 1,000 cranes will be granted a wish. Hers was for world peace.
